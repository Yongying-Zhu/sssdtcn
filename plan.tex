      \textbf{Abstract.}
      The SSSDTCN model uses a dilated causal convolution stack as its implicit
      feature extraction module, with dilation rates fixed to the exponential
      schedule $\{1,2,4,8,16,32\}$ before training. While this hand-crafted
      design achieves broad multi-scale temporal coverage, it cannot adapt to the
      specific periodicity or correlation structure of different industrial
      datasets or input segments. This document surveys and formalises
      \textbf{four principled approaches} for making the dilation factors
      \emph{adaptive}: (I)~learnable soft dilation parameters, (II)~input-conditioned
      selective kernel fusion (SKNet-inspired), (III)~frequency-spectral guided
      dilation, and (IV)~temporal self-attention guided dynamic dilation. For each
      approach the source literature, core adaptive mechanism, and essential
      mathematical formulation are presented together with an illustrative diagram.

%=============================================================================
\section*{Background}
%=============================================================================
\vspace{-6pt}

The implicit module of SSSDTCN stacks $L$ dilated causal convolution layers.
A 1-D dilated causal convolution with kernel $\wv$, kernel size $k$, and
dilation $d$ computes:
\begin{equation}
  (\xv \star_d \wv)[t] = \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \wv[i]\cdot\xv[t - d\cdot i],
  \label{eq:dconv}
\end{equation}
enforcing causality via left-only zero-padding. The current model fixes
$d_\ell = 2^{\ell-1}$, giving rates $\{1,2,4,8,16,32\}$ for $L=6$ and a
total receptive field of $\mathrm{RF} = 1+(k-1)(2^L-1)=253$ steps (for
$k=5$). Three limitations motivate adaptive designs: (1)~the Debutanizer and
SRU datasets have distinct dominant periodicities that a single fixed schedule
cannot simultaneously optimise; (2)~under high missing-ratio regimes longer
context matters more, but the schedule is mask-blind; (3)~non-stationary
operating regimes cannot be tracked by static rates.
\Cref{fig:current} illustrates the current fixed-dilation stack.

\section{Approach I: Learnable Soft Dilation Parameters}
%=============================================================================

\noindent\textit{Reference: Dai et al.\ (2017), Deformable Convolutional
Networks, ICCV; Chen et al.\ (2020), Temporal Deformable Convolution.}

\medskip

Inspired by deformable convolutions, which showed that spatial sampling
offsets can be learned from data rather than fixed on a regular grid, this
approach applies the same principle to the temporal dilation axis. Each layer
$\ell$ is assigned an unconstrained trainable scalar $\lambda_\ell \in
\mathbb{R}$; the effective dilation is $d_\ell = e^{\lambda_\ell}$, which is
always positive and differentiable. For non-integer $d_\ell$, linear
interpolation between adjacent time steps enables gradient flow back to
$\lambda_\ell$. Because $\{\lambda_\ell\}$ are optimised jointly with all
other model weights during end-to-end training, the dilation schedule converges
to the values that best fit the training data rather than to a human-specified
schedule. Once training ends the dilations are fixed; all inputs share the same
learned rates.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Core equations.}
\begin{align}
  d_\ell &= \exp(\lambda_\ell), \label{eq:softd}\\
  (\xv \star_{d_\ell} \wv)[t]
    &= \sum_{i=0}^{k-1} \wv[i]\cdot
       \underbrace{(1{-}\{d_\ell i\})\,\xv[\lfloor t-d_\ell i\rfloor]
                  +\{d_\ell i\}\,\xv[\lceil t-d_\ell i\rceil]}_{\text{linear
                  interpolation}}, \label{eq:softconv}\\
  \mathcal{L} &= \mathcal{L}_{\text{impute}}
               - \beta\!\sum_{\ell<\ell'}\!|d_\ell - d_{\ell'}|,
               \label{eq:divreg}
\end{align}
where $\{u\}$ denotes the fractional part of $u$ and the diversity penalty
\cref{eq:divreg} (weight $\beta>0$) prevents all layers collapsing to the same
rate.

\section{Approach II: Input-Conditioned Selective Kernel Dilation}
%=============================================================================

\noindent\textit{Reference: Li et al.\ (2019), Selective Kernel Networks
(SKNet), CVPR; Hu et al.\ (2018), Squeeze-and-Excitation Networks (SENet),
CVPR.}

\medskip

Approach~I's dilation rates are fixed after training and apply equally to every
input. Approach~II starts from a different premise: different inputs intrinsically
require different dilation rates, so the rates should be determined by the current
input rather than fixed in advance. The implementation proceeds as follows.
$K$~parallel dilated convolution branches with distinct candidate rates
$\{d^{(1)},\ldots,d^{(K)}\}$ are applied simultaneously to the same input,
producing $K$ feature maps. These are summed and globally average-pooled along
the time axis, compressing the entire sequence into a fixed-length vector that
captures the overall character of the current input. This vector is fed through
two fully-connected layers to produce $K$ selection weights (summing to~1, one
per branch). Finally, the $K$ feature maps are recombined as a weighted sum
using those weights, yielding the layer output. Adaptivity arises because the
weight vector is recomputed from scratch on every forward pass: a different
input yields different weights, which in turn shifts the effective dilation
toward the branch that best matches the dominant temporal scale of that input.
This is the essential difference from Approach~I---whereas Approach~I's dilations
are fixed at the end of training, Approach~II's effective dilation changes with
every new input at inference time.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Core equations.}
\begin{align}
  \tilde{\hv}^{(j)} &= \mathrm{Conv}_{d^{(j)}}\!\bigl(\hv^{(\ell-1)}\bigr),
    \quad j=1,\ldots,K, \label{eq:sk_split}\\
  \mathbf{s} &= \mathrm{AvgPool}\!\Bigl(\textstyle\sum_{j=1}^{K}
    \tilde{\hv}^{(j)}\Bigr)\in\mathbb{R}^{H}, \label{eq:sk_squeeze}\\
  \alpha^{(j)} &= \operatorname{softmax}_{j}\!\bigl(\mathbf{W}_2^{(j)}
    \operatorname{ReLU}(\mathbf{W}_1\,\mathbf{s})\bigr),
    \label{eq:sk_attn}\\
  \hv^{(\ell)} &= \sum_{j=1}^{K} \alpha^{(j)}\,\tilde{\hv}^{(j)}.
    \label{eq:sk_out}
\end{align}
The effective dilation is $d^{\mathrm{eff}} = \sum_j \alpha^{(j)} d^{(j)}$:
different inputs yield different $\alpha^{(j)}$, shifting the effective rate.

\noindent\textbf{Worked example.}\quad
With $K{=}3$ branches ($d^{(1)}{=}1$, $d^{(2)}{=}4$, $d^{(3)}{=}8$),
sequence length $T{=}100$, and hidden channels $H{=}16$: each branch
independently processes the input and outputs a $100\times16$ feature map.
Summing the three maps and averaging over the time axis yields a 16-dimensional
descriptor $\mathbf{s}$; two FC layers followed by softmax convert $\mathbf{s}$
into three weights. For a rapidly oscillating input the weights might be
$\boldsymbol{\alpha}=[0.70,\,0.20,\,0.10]$, keeping the effective dilation near
1; for a slowly varying input $\boldsymbol{\alpha}=[0.10,\,0.30,\,0.60]$ shifts
dominance to $d{=}8$. The final output is the $\alpha$-weighted sum of the three
maps, still of shape $100\times16$.

\section{Approach III: Frequency-Spectral Guided Adaptive Dilation}
%=============================================================================

\noindent\textit{Reference: Wu et al.\ (2023), TimesNet, ICLR; Zhou et al.\
(2022), FEDformer, ICML.}

\medskip

The first two approaches rely on the training process to determine dilation
rates. Approach~III involves no training at all: it reads the appropriate dilation
rates directly from the statistical properties of the data \emph{before training
begins}. Industrial time series typically contain several dominant periodic
components. The core assumption is that a convolution kernel samples most
efficiently when its stride equals one of those periods; under this assumption,
the optimal dilation for layer~$\ell$ is the one that makes the kernel's total
sampling span equal to the $\ell$-th dominant period. The procedure runs in
three steps. First, the Fourier transform is applied to all channels of the
training set and the magnitude spectra are averaged across channels, producing
a single spectrum that reflects the dataset's overall frequency structure.
Second, the $L$ largest peaks are identified, and each peak frequency is
converted to a period length by dividing the sequence length by the frequency
index. Third, for each period the required dilation is back-calculated from the
target that the kernel's sampling span equals the period: $d_\ell = \lfloor
P_\ell / (k-1)\rfloor$. These three steps execute once before training, and
the resulting dilation schedule is installed as fixed layer configuration. No
new parameters are introduced and no gradient updates are needed. The
adaptivity is \emph{dataset-level}: running the procedure on a different
dataset yields a different schedule, but within one dataset the rates are fixed
once computed. This is both the strength (zero training cost) and the
limitation (cannot track non-stationarity within the dataset).

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Core equations.}
\begin{align}
  A_f
    &= \frac{1}{C}\sum_{c=1}^{C}\bigl|\FFT(\xv[c,:])[f]\bigr|,
       \quad f = 1,\ldots,\lfloor T/2\rfloor, \label{eq:fft}\\
  f_1,\ldots,f_L
    &= \TopK\!\left(\{A_f\},\,L\right), \label{eq:topk}\\
  P_\ell
    &= \left\lfloor T/f_\ell \right\rceil, \label{eq:period}\\
  d_\ell
    &= \max\!\left(1,\;\left\lfloor P_\ell/(k-1)\right\rfloor\right).
       \label{eq:freqdil}
\end{align}

\noindent\textbf{Worked example.}\quad
With $T{=}480$, $k{=}5$, $L{=}3$: suppose the three dominant FFT peaks fall
at frequencies $f{=}20$, $f{=}8$, $f{=}4$. Applying \cref{eq:period,eq:freqdil}:

\section{Approach IV: Temporal Self-Attention Guided Dynamic Dilation}
%=============================================================================

\noindent\textit{Reference: Chen et al.\ (2020), Dynamic Convolution, CVPR;
Wang et al.\ (2018), Non-local Neural Networks, CVPR.}

\medskip

The first three approaches share a common limitation: each convolution layer
uses a single dilation rate applied uniformly to every position in the sequence.
Approach~IV breaks this restriction by computing a separate, position-specific
effective dilation for every time step within a single forward pass. Before the
convolution is applied, a lightweight self-attention module reads the layer's
input and assigns to each position~$t$ a probability distribution over candidate
offsets $\delta$. The query vector for position~$t$ is dot-produced with the
key vector of each historical position $t{-}\delta$; after softmax normalisation
these dot-products become attention weights $a_{t,\delta}$. The expected offset
$\bar{d}_t = \sum_\delta \delta\,a_{t,\delta}$ is the position's suggested
dilation: when $t$ is most similar to nearby history (high-frequency region),
weights concentrate on small $\delta$ and $\bar{d}_t$ is small; when $t$
relates more to distant history (low-frequency region), weights shift to large
$\delta$ and $\bar{d}_t$ is large. A sigmoid gate then scales the base dilation
$d_\ell^{(0)}$ by a context-dependent factor derived from $\hv^{(\ell-1)}$,
bounding the final rate within $[0,\,2d_\ell^{(0)}]$ to prevent the receptive
field from growing without bound. This computation is repeated independently
for every position~$t$, making Approach~IV the finest-grained of all four
methods. The cost is that the attention computation scales linearly with
sequence length, making inference noticeably more expensive than the other
approaches.

\medskip

\noindent\textbf{Core equations.}
\begin{align}
  e_{t,\delta}
    &= \frac{\mathbf{q}_t^{\top}\mathbf{k}_{t-\delta}}{\sqrt{d_k}},
    \qquad
    a_{t,\delta}
    = \frac{\exp(e_{t,\delta})}{\sum_{\delta'}\exp(e_{t,\delta'})},
    \label{eq:attn}\\
  \bar{d}_t
    &= \sum_{\delta=0}^{T_{\max}} \delta\cdot a_{t,\delta},
    \label{eq:effd}\\
  \tilde{d}_{t,\ell}
    &= d_\ell^{(0)}\cdot 2\,\sigmoid\!\bigl(\mathbf{w}_s^{\top}
       \hv^{(\ell-1)}[:,t]+b_s\bigr),
    \label{eq:dyngate}
\end{align}
where \cref{eq:dyngate} constrains the adapted dilation to
$[0,\,2d_\ell^{(0)}]$.

\section{Comparative Discussion}
%=============================================================================

\Cref{tab:comparison} summarises the four approaches along key design
dimensions. For a practical starting point, a hybrid of Approaches~III and~I
is recommended: use the spectral calibration (\cref{eq:freqdil}) to initialise
$\lambda_\ell^{(0)} = \log(d_\ell^{\text{spectral}})$, then fine-tune with
learnable soft dilations. This combines physics-guided initialisation with
end-to-end refinement at minimal additional cost.
